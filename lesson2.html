Lesson 1: Blockchain Architecture and Components

Blockchain technology is a revolutionary decentralized system that ensures transparency, security, and immutability. The blockchain architecture consists of multiple layers, each playing a crucial role in its functionality.

The key components of blockchain include:

  • Blocks: A block contains multiple transactions that are verified and added to the chain.
  • Transactions: These represent changes in the blockchain's state, recording exchanges of assets, data, or smart contract executions.
  • Ledger: A distributed and decentralized database that stores the entire history of transactions, making it transparent and tamper-proof.
  • Nodes: Independent computers in the network that validate and maintain the blockchain.

Lesson 2: Consensus Algorithms and Their Importance

In a decentralized network, reaching agreement on the state of the blockchain is crucial. Consensus algorithms are the mechanisms that allow nodes to agree on valid transactions.

Here are some of the most common consensus mechanisms:

  • Proof of Work (PoW): Used by Bitcoin, it requires solving complex mathematical puzzles to validate transactions. Highly secure but energy-intensive.
  • Proof of Stake (PoS): Validators are chosen based on their stake (number of tokens held). More energy-efficient but can lead to wealth centralization.
  • Delegated Proof of Stake (DPoS): Token holders vote for delegates who validate transactions, improving scalability.
  • Practical Byzantine Fault Tolerance (PBFT): Ensures consensus even with malicious nodes, commonly used in permissioned blockchains.

Lesson 3: Smart Contracts and Their Role in Blockchain

A smart contract is a self-executing contract where the agreement terms are written in code. It automates transactions and removes the need for intermediaries.

Key features of smart contracts:

  • Automation: Transactions are executed automatically when predefined conditions are met.
  • Security: Running on blockchain ensures transparency and immutability.
  • Efficiency: Reduces costs and eliminates manual verification.

Smart contracts are used in multiple industries, such as:

  • Finance: Automating loan agreements and payments.
  • Real Estate: Managing property transfers with reduced paperwork.
  • Supply Chain: Ensuring transparent tracking of goods.

However, smart contracts are not without risks. Bugs in the contract code can lead to security vulnerabilities. Auditing and testing smart contracts before deployment is crucial to prevent potential losses.
